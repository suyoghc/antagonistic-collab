# Posterior Collapse: Likelihood Tempering vs. Prediction Noise

**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Context:** M6 arbiter-v0.1 live validation revealed posterior collapse as the primary architectural bottleneck. The Bayesian posterior concentrates to P~1.0 after 1-2 experiments, making later cycles uninformative (EIG=0). This document compares two approaches to slowing collapse.

---

## 1. The Problem

Our system uses binomial log-likelihood to update a posterior over three cognitive models (GCM, SUSTAIN, RULEX). With `n_subjects=20` and ~10 items per experiment, a single experiment generates ~10 nats of discriminating evidence — a probability ratio of ~22,000:1. After two experiments the log-odds reach ~50 nats (ratio ~ 5x10^21) and the posterior is functionally a delta.

This happens because:
- Synthetic data is generated by one model *exactly* — no model misspecification
- Binomial likelihood is sharp: small prediction differences produce large log-likelihood gaps
- Evidence accumulates multiplicatively in log space across items
- No entropy floor, dampening, or convergence check exists in the pipeline

In M6 live validation: GCM and SUSTAIN locked to the correct model on cycle 0. RULEX took until cycle 2 (non-monotonic trajectory — initially favored the wrong model, then self-corrected). After collapse, crux negotiation identifies decisive questions but EIG=0 means there's nothing to boost.

---

## 2. Likelihood Tempering (Power Posteriors)

### The operation

Raise the likelihood to a fractional power tau in (0, 1]:

```
log posterior = log prior + tau * log likelihood
```

In the binomial case, this is exactly equivalent to reducing n_subjects:

```
L(data | model)^tau = Binom(k, n, p)^tau ~ Binom(tau*k, tau*n, p)
```

Tempering with tau=0.1 on n_subjects=20 gives the same posterior update as n_subjects=2.

### Properties

- **Deterministic**: same posterior every run
- **Order-preserving**: if model A has higher likelihood than model B, tempering preserves this. The gap shrinks but the ranking never flips.
- **Single parameter**: tau controls the rate of evidence accumulation
- **Clean interpretation**: "I'm acting as if I have less data" or "I trust this evidence at fraction tau"
- **Theoretically grounded**: unique coherent update under the generalized Bayesian framework (Bissiri et al. 2016)

### Literature

**Three independent intellectual lineages converge on the same operation:**

#### Generalized Bayesian Inference ("learning rate" eta)

Grunwald (2012), "The Safe Bayesian," introduced the idea that standard Bayesian updating is over-confident under model misspecification. The fix: replace the likelihood with L^eta where eta in (0, 1] is a *learning rate* adapted to the data. When the model is correct, eta -> 1 recovers standard Bayes. When misspecified, eta < 1 prevents the posterior from concentrating on a wrong answer.

- **Grunwald, P. (2012).** The Safe Bayesian. *Algorithmic Learning Theory (ALT 2012)*, Lecture Notes in Computer Science 7568, pp. 169-183. Springer.
  https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-642-34106-9_16

- **Bissiri, P. G., Holmes, C. C., & Walker, S. G. (2016).** A General Framework for Updating Belief Distributions. *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series B*, 78(5), 1103-1130.
  https://arxiv.org/abs/1306.6430
  *Key result:* This is the *only* coherent way to update beliefs via a general loss function, with standard Bayes recovered as a special case.

- **Knoblauch, J., Jewson, J., & Damoulas, T. (2022).** An Optimization-Centric View on Bayes' Rule: Reviewing and Generalizing Variational Inference. *Journal of Machine Learning Research*, 23(132), 1-73.
  https://jmlr.org/papers/v23/19-1047.html
  *Key result:* Comprehensive framework unifying tempered posteriors, variational inference, and generalized Bayes.

#### Robust Bayesian Inference ("coarsened posterior")

Miller & Dunson (2019) showed that conditioning on "the model is approximately correct" (rather than exactly correct) produces a posterior that can be approximated by simply raising the likelihood to a fractional power. The c-posterior improves robustness with essentially no additional computational cost.

- **Miller, J. W. & Dunson, D. B. (2019).** Robust Bayesian Inference via Coarsening. *Journal of the American Statistical Association*, 114(527), 1113-1125.
  https://arxiv.org/abs/1506.06101
  *Key result:* When closeness is defined in terms of relative entropy, the "coarsened" posterior is approximated by the power posterior. This is the closest theoretical justification for tempering in our setting — our synthetic data is "too correct."

#### Power Posteriors for Model Comparison

Friel & Pettitt (2008) and O'Hagan (1995) introduced power posteriors and fractional Bayes factors as tools for marginal likelihood estimation. The path from prior to posterior is traversed gradually by increasing the likelihood exponent from 0 to 1.

- **Friel, N. & Pettitt, A. N. (2008).** Marginal Likelihood Estimation via Power Posteriors. *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series B*, 70(3), 589-607.
  https://rss.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9868.2007.00650.x

- **O'Hagan, A. (1995).** Fractional Bayes Factors for Model Comparison. *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series B*, 57(1), 99-138.
  https://rss.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.2517-6161.1995.tb02017.x

## 3. Adding Noise to Model Predictions

### The operation

Before computing the likelihood, perturb each model's predicted probability:

```
p_noisy_i = clip(p_i + epsilon_i, 0.01, 0.99)    where epsilon ~ N(0, sigma^2)
log L_noisy = sum_i log Binom(k_i, n, p_noisy_i)
```

### Properties

- **Stochastic**: different runs produce different posteriors
- **Not order-preserving**: noise can flip which model has higher likelihood on individual items. A model predicting p=0.82 for an item with observed accuracy 0.80 might get a worse likelihood than a model predicting p=0.70, if noise pushes the first to 0.95 and the second to 0.75.
- **Two parameters**: noise distribution (shape, scale) and application point (per-item vs per-model)
- **No clean interpretation**: our models *aren't* uncertain — GCM.predict() is deterministic. The noise would be fabricated, not derived from anything in the model.
- **No theoretical backing**: not derived from any axiomatic framework for belief updating

### The physical intuition is correct

The intuition behind adding prediction noise is sound: in reality, cognitive models don't perfectly predict human behavior. There are individual differences in attention, learning rate, response strategies. There's measurement noise, model misspecification, unmodeled context effects. Real data would be noisier than our synthetic data.

But this is exactly what the generalized Bayes literature formalizes. Grunwald's argument: when your model is misspecified (or, in our case, *too well-specified* because the data comes from the exact model), standard Bayesian updating is over-confident. The principled correction is to scale the learning rate, not to add noise to predictions. Miller & Dunson (2019) proved that tempering is the correct approximation to "conditioning on the model being approximately correct."

## 4. Head-to-Head Comparison

| Property | Likelihood Tempering | Prediction Noise |
|---|---|---|
| **Deterministic** | Yes | No |
| **Preserves model ordering** | Yes — gap shrinks, ranking preserved | No — individual items can flip |
| **Parameters** | 1 (tau) | 2+ (distribution, scale) |
| **Interpretation** | "Less data" / "trust evidence at fraction tau" | "Models are uncertain about predictions" |
| **Theoretical basis** | Generalized Bayes (Bissiri 2016), Safe Bayes (Grunwald 2012), Coarsened posterior (Miller & Dunson 2019) | None (ad hoc) |
| **Equivalent to** | Reducing n_subjects by factor tau | No clean equivalence |
| **Reproducibility** | Identical across runs | Varies across runs (need to marginalize) |
| **Effect on collapse** | Slows uniformly | Slows stochastically, can reverse |

### Mathematical relationship

In the binomial case, tempering and reducing n_subjects are exactly equivalent:

```
Binom(k, n, p)^tau ~ Binom(tau*k, tau*n, p)
```

So tau=0.1 with n=20 is the same as n=2. This makes the operation interpretable: "I'm acting as if I observed 2 subjects, not 20."

Adding noise has no such clean equivalence. If you wanted a principled noise-based approach, the standard tool is a **beta-binomial likelihood** (overdispersion model):

```
p(k | n, p, alpha) = BetaBinomial(k; n, alpha*p, alpha*(1-p))
```

This models heterogeneity across subjects (some attend more, some less) rather than just "less confidence." It's strictly more informative than tempering because it captures the *structure* of uncertainty. But it requires calibrating alpha, is more complex, and the current system doesn't model individual differences.

## 5. When Each Approach is Right

### Use tempering when:
- Data is synthetic (no real model misspecification to model)
- The problem is purely that evidence is too strong
- You want a simple, deterministic, theoretically grounded fix
- Backward compatibility matters (tau=1.0 recovers current behavior)

**This is our current situation.**

### Use prediction noise / beta-binomial when:
- Data comes from real human subjects (individual differences are real)
- You want to model a *specific source* of uncertainty (attention variability, response noise)
- The overdispersion parameter alpha can be calibrated from pilot data

**This is the future situation (AutoRA/Prolific integration).**

### Use both when:
- Real data with known model misspecification
- Tempering for "I don't fully trust my model" + beta-binomial for "subjects vary"

## 6. Recommended Implementation

### For M7: Likelihood tempering

Add a `learning_rate` parameter (tau) to `ModelPosterior.update()`:

```python
def update(self, log_likelihoods: np.ndarray, learning_rate: float = 1.0):
    self.log_probs = self.log_probs + learning_rate * log_likelihoods
    self.log_probs = self.log_probs - self.log_probs.max()
```

Propagate through `update_posterior_from_experiment()` and `run_execution()`. Default tau=1.0 for backward compatibility. Recommended tau=0.1-0.3 for synthetic data.

**Why tau=0.1-0.3?** With n_subjects=20 and ~10 items, one experiment generates ~10 nats. At tau=0.1, this becomes ~1 nat per experiment. After 5 cycles: ~5 nats total, giving a probability ratio of ~150:1 — still decisive, but the posterior doesn't collapse until cycle 3-4, leaving room for crux negotiation and structural variation to matter.

### For future (real data): Beta-binomial likelihood

Replace `binom.logpmf` in `compute_log_likelihood()` with `betabinom.logpmf`. Calibrate alpha from pilot data or use a weakly informative default (alpha=10 gives moderate overdispersion).

## 7. Connection to ADO Literature

Cavagnaro, Myung & Pitt developed Adaptive Design Optimization (ADO) — sequential Bayesian experimental design for model discrimination in cognitive science, applied to exactly the same domain (competing cognitive models of categorization). Their system uses mutual information (equivalent to our EIG) to select experiments.

- **Cavagnaro, D. R., Myung, J. I., Pitt, M. A., & Kujala, J. V. (2010).** Adaptive Design Optimization: A Mutual Information-Based Approach to Model Discrimination in Cognitive Science. *Neural Computation*, 22(4), 887-905.
  https://direct.mit.edu/neco/article-abstract/22/4/887/7521

- **Myung, J. I., Cavagnaro, D. R., & Pitt, M. A. (2013).** A Tutorial on Adaptive Design Optimization. *Journal of Mathematical Psychology*, 57(3-4), 53-67.
  https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3755632/

- **Ryan, E. G., Drovandi, C. C., McGree, J. M., & Pettitt, A. N. (2016).** A Review of Modern Computational Algorithms for Bayesian Optimal Design. *International Statistical Review*, 84(1), 128-154.
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/insr.12107

**Key observation:** Cavagnaro et al. don't discuss posterior collapse because their systems run with *real human subjects* — noisy data naturally prevents collapse. Our system collapses faster than any real experiment would because synthetic data from a known generative model provides perfectly discriminating evidence. Tempering corrects for this unrealistic cleanliness.

---

## 8. Summary

Tempering and prediction noise both slow posterior collapse. They are related (both flatten the likelihood surface) but not equivalent:

- **Tempering** is the principled, deterministic, order-preserving, theoretically grounded approach. It's equivalent to reducing effective sample size. One parameter, clean interpretation, identical results across runs.

- **Prediction noise** is ad hoc, stochastic, can flip model rankings, and has no axiomatic basis. The correct formalization of the intuition behind it is either tempering (Grunwald, Miller & Dunson) or the beta-binomial likelihood (overdispersion modeling).

For synthetic data: use tempering. For real human data: use beta-binomial. The physical intuition behind noise is correct — real experiments are noisier — but tempering captures this more cleanly than injecting fabricated noise into deterministic model predictions.
